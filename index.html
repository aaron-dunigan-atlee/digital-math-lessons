Aaron Dunigan AtLee's Digital Math Lessons

Some activities highlighting what I've done to enhance high-school math instruction using instructional technology.

You will need The Geometer's Sketchpad or Fathom to open some of the files here. GeoGebra sketches should open in the browser.

Horizontal Translations

GeoGebra

Explanation

A sketch for demonstrating why the graph of y=f(x+k) translates in the opposite direction to the sign of k. (Honestly, I couldn't really answer that question until I built this sketch.)

Derivatives Roller Coaster

GeoGebra

Explanation

Students learn intuitive concepts about derivatives by thinking about the direction and speed of a roller coaster that rides along the function's graph. "If you can imagine riding a roller coaster, you can understand calculus."

Introduction to Sinusoidal Functions

Instructions

Students discover the shape and properties of sinusoidal functions by plotting periodic data related to sunrise times.

Investigate Graphs of Sinusoidal Functions

Instructions

Students explore the impact of the parameters on the shape of sine and cosine graphs.

Investigate Sound Waves

GeoGebra

Instructions

After a unit about sine and cosine graphs, students apply their knowledge to analyze the graph of a sound wave generated in class with a tuning fork. Students use the results to determine if the tuning fork is in tune. The GeoGebra sketch shows a sample result.

Investigate Derivatives

Instructions

Students investigate the idea of a derivative as "a function that measures the slope (gradient) of another function." They discover empirically the rule for writing the derivative of a polynomial.

Discovering Vertex Form

Instructions

Students derive the formulae for the axis of symmetry and vertex of a quadratic equation, by considering the average of the solutions to f(x)=c. Then they explore the graph of the vertex form of a quadratic.

Basketball Parabolas

Student Sketch (GeoGebra)

Completed Example (GeoGebra)

Instructions

Students use a video of a basketball shot to mark points and develop an equation, in order to predict whether the shot will go in.

The Theory Behind a Chi-squared Test for Independence

Fathom

Explanation

A Fathom demonstration for explaining the theory behind the chi-squared test for independence. The Fathom file has interactive features, but in case you don't have Fathom, the explanation is a static copy of the Fathom file.

Investigate Factors and Intercepts in Quadratics

Instructions

Students investigate how the factored form of a quadratic function relates to the x-intercepts, which in turn is useful in solving a quadratic equation in one variable. Students develop some concepts that lead to a discussion of the zero-product property and solving by factorization.

Vertical Line Test

Sketchpad

An animated demonstration of the vertical line test.

Irrational vs. Rational

Sketchpad

Instructions

Students are expected to classify numbers, a task that usually lacks any meaningful context. I wanted them to really "feel" the difference between rational and irrational in a physical way, rather than the common "typographic" understanding most students have ("a decimal that neither repeats nor terminates"). This was my attempt to build this understanding. It had mixed results, but I think it was a promising start.

Polynomial Graph Exploration

Excel

Instructions

Wow! A blast from the past: before I'd even heard of Sketchpad or GeoGebra or Desmos, I was making sliders in Excel! Students manipulate sliders for the coefficients to discover various properties of polynomial graphs.

Rectangle with Fixed Perimeter

Sketchpad

Instructions

Okay, I know this idea has been around the block a few times, but here's my take on the classic question of maximizing the area of a rectangle with fixed perimeter. There's still some great math to be got from this, including circling back to it after discovering calculus and thinking once more about optimization.

Slinky

GeoGebra

Mostly just for fun. My IB Math HL class studied linear parametric equations in three dimensions. I wanted them to see what could be done with non-linear parametric equations. GeoGebra has an option to display this so it can be viewed with red-blue 3D glasses. What fun!
